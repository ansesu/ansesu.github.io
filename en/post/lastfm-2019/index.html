My 2019 last.fm

Andre
Jul 18, 2020 7 min read

last.fm is a social network in which users can keep track of their own musical activity through the so called scrobbles (the log of your listened song). This service was created in 2002, when it was necessary to install a software in order to scrobble. Recently, however, the plataform was integrated to music streaming services such as Spotify, Deezer, Tidal and Google Play Music. Thus, it has become much easier to keep track of your own musical activity. last.fm shows weekly (in the free version), monthly and annual (in the premium version) statistics, concerning the number of listened songs, artists, albums, genres, temporal patterns and comparison with other last.fm users. In addition, the plataform allows users to find people with similar musical tastes.

Here, I perform a statistical analysis of my last.fm profile in 2019 (and who knows turn this an annual thing). I start by using this tool to export my profile’s listening history. Then, I use Python language to parse data and make some visualizations. My code is available at: https://github.com/ansesu/lastfm/. So, here we go! First of all, some numbers that summarize my 2019.

Next, I applied the concept of Shannon entropy to the distribution of artists listened. The normalized Shanon entropy is described by the equation [1] $$ H = \text{Diversity} = -\dfrac{1}{\log n}\sum_i P_i \log P_i $$ where $n$ is the total number of artists and $P_i$ is the fraction of songs from the $i$th artist. The entropy indicates the quantity of information contained in the probability distribution of artists. Values close to one ($\approx 1$) reveal that information is equally distributed among artists or, in other words, I listen to them in the same proportion. Conversely, values close to zero ($\approx 0$) indicate that one of the artists dominates my musical sessions. Therefore, I consider that the entropy is a kind of musical diversity (or heterogeinity) considering the artists I listen, which is different than being eclectic. The figure below shows the entropy for each week in 2019. The entropy exhibit smaller values exactly when I had my longest streaks. Also, it seems that, during a week, I tend to listen to a set of artists homogeneously.

Figure: Diversity of artist (as measured by Shannon entropy) in each week of 2019.

The figure below shows the entropy for each day of the week in 2019. Weekdays present smaller values of entropy, what may suggest that I tend to listen to a specific kind of music (and consequently of artist) when I am performing my activities.

Figure: Diversity of artist (as measured by Shannon entropy) in each day of the week in 2019.

Browsing the internet, I found a cool visualization called stream wave chart. This kind of figure shows the temporal evolution of the volume of songs listened (given by a filled area) of a set of specific artists. I found it really awesome and tried to make my own stream chart. The above figures were all made using the Python package Bokeh. But it turns out this kind of visualization is not available on Bokeh. I searched for some alternatives and found out the Altair package (also from Python). Then, I selected all the artists with at least 30 reproductions in 2019, which yielded a set of 21 unique artists. The figure below shows the evolution of my reproduction volume in 2019. You can click on the names to select a specific artist. Also, you can do your own last.fm wave chart in a non-pythonic way by using this site (try it out!).

Figure: Temporal evolution of the volume of reproductions of top artists in 2019.
